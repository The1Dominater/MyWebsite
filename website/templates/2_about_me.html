When I started my degree in Computer Science at Regis University, all I really wanted was to make Minecraft mods and only went to school based on my parents' advice. After getting past the introductory courses, my interest peaked while taking classes like computational theory, operating systems, and paradigms of programming languages.

Realizing Regis's Computer Science department was limited by its size, I transferred to CU Boulder to gain access to a greater variety of courses and teaching experience. At CU I took introductory courses in quantum computing, robotics, cyber-security, and bio-networking. However, I enjoyed system administration and data center computing the most because they involved problem solving and managing systems at a massive scale(Simply put [Super Computers == cool] evaluates to True).

During my senior year at CU, I worked with FESTO and senior project team to develop a software based system for generating unique ids for a specific device based on irreplicable defects in the device's memory. The idea was to pull initialized binary values from the device's memory on startup and plug them into a Physically Unclonable Function(PUF) in order to generate a unique id which could only be generated by the given device. Then it would send the id to a web based Solidity contract where a business or organization could grant the device access to their service(s) or system(s). Most recently, I interned with the National Renewable Energy Laboratory through the SULI program. I worked within the Power Systems Engineering Center to help develop a LLM for analyzing power system questions a utility company may face and assist the utility operators in addressing the problem. My mentor, Rishab Jain, and I focused on developing an multi-agent AI system where each AI agent was an expert in a specific domain, such as system maintenance, cost analysis, and human comfort. The expert agents then would bounce ideas off each other in an attempt to build a more robust and thought out solution.
